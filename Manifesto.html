Are You Green?

What Do We Stand For

Design should be made honest. It should not claim to be something else nor should it appear or suggest to be more than it is. This statement is taken in the perspective of sustainability and specifically sustainable packaging, material or product with reference to green washing. Green washing takes many forms that some may not realize.

  1. Design should most and for most not lie to the consumers.
  2. Design should not try to exaggerate its green effect by emphasizing one sustainable change made.
  3. Design should not be made purposefully vague in order to imply it is something it is not.
  4. Design should not simply add green symbols and words to evoke the green sensation.
  5. Design should not just add sustainable material for the sake of appearing more sustainable.

As a designer working for the interest of a company, green washing would not only hurt the company in the present but also the future. In the present, though the average consumer may not think to look deeper into the topic, others will. In the case of coca cola many articles have been written about their exaggeration and emphasis on their marine bottles contradicting their current pollution contribution, ruing the public image of the company. In the skims case, many vocal consumers speak out and create backlash for the company with statements like 'It's time SKIMS 'gets its ass up' and starts disclosing more information about how, where, and by whom its items are produced, as well as the materials used.' Other shoppers made regards such as “As a shopper, you have the right to now how SKIMS' production practices impact the world and its inhabitants' [Javed]. With the current day and age of social media, backlash can “damage a company's reputation, hurt sales, and even lead to legal action” ["How Social”]. The form of deceit seen in all the examples above “erodes trust” within the consumer [Shreenath].

Using this tactic would also inhibit future sustainable growth of the company. As they take this to be enough, active incentive to research and develop better alternative methods are not taken or can be delayed [Shreenath]. Study shows that “66% of consumers are willing to spend more money on sustainable products, and... people are willing to pay as much as 25% more for greener alternatives” [”Do Consumers”]. This is most likely already known, which is why greenwashing occurs. However, the greenwashing method may have worked for millennials (being twice as likely to reduce their environmental impact compared to boomers), but “Gen Z is soon to become the next dominant generation, with many being more concerned about sustainability than millennials” [”Do Consumers”]. As already seen with the numerous amount of knowledge and articles spread about greenwashing, these methods will not be enough in the future. As the society moves toward increasing interest in green purchases, the lack of action to truly become green will set the company behind.
