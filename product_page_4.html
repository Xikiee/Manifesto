Capsule

This packaging design is inspired by nivea and toothpaste brands which are seen to over packaging their product just to conceal the plastic container [Krishna]. Giving credit where necessary, in some cases this is done to store important information about the product such as the ingredients and or warnings. While in this case the use of a cardboard box is better then using another layer of plastic, the best solution would be the use of a singular packaging layer. It is instead possible to write a short disclaimer on the products physicals packaging (in this case the actual bottle). Additionally, a qr code can printed on the packaging which when scanned will redirect the consumer to a web page about the product. In cases where it is legally required to provide the extensive amount of warnings physically, (such as for medical products), it is understandable for this practice to be in use. However for cases such as lotions or toothpaste, this overpackaging is simply overkill.

The appeal for this in the perspective of a designer, is that studies shown that people are more likely to purchase a product where the packaging is made from a paper like material. This is evident from a survey done by Ipsos where “seven in ten (71%) say they are more likely to buy brands that package products in paper or cardboard that in other materials” [ipsos]. Similarly, in a series of 8 studies done across the US, UK and the Netherlands show that “consumers will perceive a product's packaging as more environmentally friendly if it's plastic with an additional layer of paper than if the product is visibly packaged in identical plastic packaging but without the paper”[Krishna]. However this practice is more costly on the producers as more money is being invested into unnecessary packaging. Not to mention the extra space taken up by the box may make transportation less efficient and more costly. Other negative impacts with respect to customer view is further explained on the home page.
